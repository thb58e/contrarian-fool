Investment Philosophy

Buying a stock is buying a piece of a business. Nothing more, nothing less.

When stock ownership is viewed as business ownership‚Äîwith the intention to hold for many years‚Äîimportant questions naturally arise:

‚ùØ Circle of Competence

Can the business be clearly understood? If not, it should be avoided.

‚ùØ Business Model & Moat

How does the company make money? Who are the customers? How long can those earnings last?
The strongest businesses:

  • Earn durable cash flows across market cycles
  • Deliver genuine value to users
  • Rely on simple, understandable models‚Äînot financial complexity
  • Encourage repeat purchases through habit, satisfaction, or necessity
  • Enjoy high customer loyalty and pricing power

The goal is to find businesses that can become "friends of time," compounding quietly over the years.

‚ùØ Management & Culture

Is the founder a long-term thinker with sincerity and vision?
Important considerations include:

  • A culture focused on user value, not shortcuts
  • Systems that respect human nature and empower employees
  • A leadership team that aligns incentives with long-term results
  • High employee retention, intrinsic motivation, and a shared sense of purpose
  • An organization capable of doing the right thing even when it's hard

‚ùØ Margin of Safety

What is the business worth?
Avoid overpaying. Price matters‚Äîespecially when future cash flows are being bought with today's capital.
