About

I've been investing professionally for over a decade. What keeps me going is a deep love for the craft of long-term investing—and a strong belief in the timeless principles of Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger. I also draw inspiration from a few modern-day investors I admire deeply: Li Lu at Himalaya, Chris Hohn at TCI, and Duan Yongping.

People often ask, "What stock should I buy?" But I think that's the wrong question. A better one is: Which business should I own? That shift—from stock picker to business owner—makes a world of difference.

I started this blog to collect my thoughts: lessons learned, mistakes made, and ideas I'm still wrestling with. By sharing these reflections, I hope to be useful to others making investment decisions—especially those focused on preserving and growing their hard-earned wealth across generations. The kind of decisions I'd want my own family to make.

To me, investing should be simple—but never easy. It's about deeply understanding the businesses you own, having the patience to let them grow, staying true to your values, and tuning out the noise of short-term thinking or chasing quick wins. Easier said than done.

Yes, great investing means finding the right business at the right price. But just as important—perhaps even harder—is avoiding the mistakes that can set you back years. I focus on owning high-quality businesses with durable growth potential and try to ignore the market's daily distractions.

In short, I believe in owning great businesses—not chasing stock tickers.

A few reminders I live by:

  • Only invest in what I understand.
  • Buying stocks means buying ownership in real businesses. No more, no less.
  • Do the right things for the long term.
  • Invert: avoid what can hurt you.
  • Fast is slow. Slow can be fastest.
  • Stay rational. Ignore the sirens of short-term thinking.
  • Be patient. Always.
  • Fix mistakes quickly—however painful it feels now, it's the cheapest price you'll pay.

This blog is a place to share thoughts, principles, and perspectives—not investment recommendations.

Thanks for visiting. If you believe in long-term thinking, business fundamentals, durable wealth, and lifelong learning—welcome. You might be in good company.

Investment Philosophy

  1. Buy Businesses, Not Stocks
    View stocks as partial ownership of real businesses.
    Buying a stock is buying a piece of a business. Nothing more, nothing less.
    "Buying a stock is buying the company's discounted future cash flows."
  2. Stay Within Your Circle of Competence
    Only invest in companies you deeply understand.
    "If you don't understand it, don't touch it."
    Avoid complexity and hype — simplicity is strength.
  3. Do the Right Thing + Do Things Right ("Benfen")
    This is my core principle:
    "Do the right thing" = act ethically, invest in great businesses.
    "Do things right" = execute patiently and carefully.
    Success comes from discipline, integrity, and self-awareness, not shortcuts.
  4. Long-Term Thinking Over Short-Term Gains
    Avoid trying to make fast money — that mindset leads to mistakes.
    Great companies take time to show results.
    "Many people are poor because they want to get rich quickly."
  5. Focus on Moats (Durable Competitive Advantages)
    Invest in companies with pricing power, strong brands, or network effects.
    Businesses that can raise prices and still retain customers are likely to have a wide moat.
  6. Be Price Sensitive, But Not Obsessive
    A great business at a fair price is better than a mediocre business at a low price.
    Avoid market timing; instead, wait patiently for the right price to buy.
  7. Rationality Over Emotion
    Stay calm during volatility. Don't get swayed by market euphoria or fear.
    Check and revisit your investment thesis instead of reacting impulsively.
  8. Invest with High Conviction
    Concentrated bets are fine — if you're within your competence zone.
    "Put all your eggs in one basket — and watch that basket closely."
  9. Avoid Overtrading
    Frequent buying and selling leads to poor decisions and higher costs.
    Hold great businesses for the long run.
  10. Keep Learning & Reflecting
    Be humble. Review and learn from your mistakes.
    Continuously refine your understanding of businesses, economics, and human nature.
