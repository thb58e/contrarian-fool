What is the single most important thing in life and investing?

If you polled a hundred people, you might hear "talent," "diligence," or "education." These are important, of course. It's none of that. The most important things is doing the right things—and then strive to do them right consistently. On the other side of the coin is avoiding doing the wrong things.

I once had a close friend who was an exceptionally diligent person. He put in long hours at work, stayed up late researching stocks, and spent every weekend reading about companies. Despite this, he frequently switched jobs, flitted from one "hot" investment to another, and chased the next big thing every time the news made a fuss. After several years, he realised that no matter how many hours he clocked in, he was basically spinning his wheels and seeing minimal results.

It wasn't a lack of talent or effort. He was simply focusing on immediate, short-term wins—often driven by news or social pressure—rather than taking the time to confirm he was doing the right thing for his long-term goals. Now compare this to investors who stick to their convictions, even when it looks foolish in the moment. Over ten or twenty years, those small, methodical steps compound into massive leaps forward.

It's like traveling: no matter how fast you drive, if you're heading in the wrong direction, you won't reach your intended destination. The power of "do the right thing" is all about checking your map—your thesis—and making sure you're still on the correct path. If you discover a mistake, correct your course right away. This approach saves you enormous time and effort over an investing lifetime.

Why Short-Term Thinking Derails Long-Term Wealth

We live in a world obsessed with quick results. Look at social media: Everyone's touting the next meme stock or the day's best cryptocurrency trade. Nobody wants to hear, "I'm planning to hold this blue-chip company for thirty years"—they want a hot stock tip that'll triple in thirty days.

Yet short-term thinking usually leads to subpar results:

  • Fear of missing out (FOMO) prompts people to buy into rallies at inflated prices.
  • Panic drives them to sell solid investments at a loss when volatility hits.
  • Boredom makes them trade excessively, racking up fees and emotional stress.

I remember once attending a local investor meetup where nearly everyone was talking about a single hype-driven company. "This time is different," they insisted. "It can only go up!" People were convinced it was the ticket to overnight riches. Fast-forward a few months: the stock plummeted. Most of those short-term speculators lost their shirts, while the conversation quickly shifted to the next big thing.

Meanwhile, a quiet, gray-haired gentleman in the corner had been talking about a simple, boring, high-quality business. I later discovered he'd been investing for decades, and his steady, measured approach had let him retire comfortably in his 50s. He wasn't flashy, but he slept well at night—and his portfolio beat the market handily over the long run.

Doing the Right Thing for the Long Haul

So, how do you put "doing the right thing" into practice?

  • Identify your direction. In investing, this means finding a strategy—value investing, dividend growth, long-term compounders, or a different well-researched approach—and sticking to it.
  • Check your map. If new information comes to light and shows you're heading the wrong way, correct course quickly. Don't cling to mistakes.
  • Focus on the process, not just outcomes. Short-term price action is mostly noise. Instead, evaluate whether your decisions align with your goals and your strategy's core principles.
  • Look decades ahead. It's corny, but thinking about how you'll view today's choices in ten or twenty years is a powerful filter for decisions.

When you approach life and investing this way, you may feel "behind" those chasing the latest fad for instant gains. But over time, you'll discover they often end up stuck or running in circles—while you've taken consistent steps in the right direction, building momentum that compounds.
