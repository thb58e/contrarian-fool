Investment Philosophy

Invest in what you understand, stay disciplined, think long term, and let great businesses compound quietly.

  1. Buy Businesses, Not Stocks
    View stocks as partial ownership of real businesses.
    Focus on long-term intrinsic value, not short-term price movements.
    "Buying a stock is buying the company's discounted future cash flows."
  2. Stay Within Your Circle of Competence
    Only invest in companies you deeply understand.
    "If you don't understand it, don't touch it."
    Avoid complexity and hype — simplicity is strength.
  3. Do the Right Thing + Do Things Right ("Benfen")
    This is my core principle:
    "Do the right thing" = act ethically, invest in great businesses.
    "Do things right" = execute patiently and carefully.
    Success comes from discipline, integrity, and self-awareness, not shortcuts.
  4. Long-Term Thinking Over Short-Term Gains
    Avoid trying to make fast money — that mindset leads to mistakes.
    Great companies take time to show results.
    "Many people are poor because they want to get rich quickly."
  5. Focus on Moats (Durable Competitive Advantages)
    Invest in companies with pricing power, strong brands, or network effects.
    Businesses that can raise prices and still retain customers are likely to have a wide moat.
  6. Be Price Sensitive, But Not Obsessive
    A great business at a fair price is better than a mediocre business at a low price.
    Avoid market timing; instead, wait patiently for the right price to buy.
  7. Rationality Over Emotion
    Stay calm during volatility. Don't get swayed by market euphoria or fear.
    Check and revisit your investment thesis instead of reacting impulsively.
  8. Invest with High Conviction
    Concentrated bets are fine — if you're within your competence zone.
    "Put all your eggs in one basket — and watch that basket closely."
  9. Avoid Overtrading
    Frequent buying and selling leads to poor decisions and higher costs.
    Hold great businesses for the long run.
  10. Keep Learning & Reflecting
    Be humble. Review and learn from your mistakes.
    Continuously refine your understanding of businesses, economics, and human nature.
